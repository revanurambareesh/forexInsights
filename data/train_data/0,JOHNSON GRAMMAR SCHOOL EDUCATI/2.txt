', Boris Johnson',  has become the latest senior Tory to support selective education, giving his backing to grammar schools during his LBC Radio phone-in.', 
', The London mayor, who is poised to return to Westminster after being ', selected as the Conservative party candidate at the next election for Uxbridge and South Ruislip', , described the decision to scrap grammar schools as \u201ca real tragedy for this country\u201d. \u201cI think they are a very important part of the mix in our educational system. I think that they work very well in many areas and they should be supported,\u201d he said.', 
', His comments follow an intervention last week by the home secretary, Theresa May, who backed the possibility of a new grammar school in her Maidenhead constituency in Berkshire, despite the fact that current legislation bans the creation of selective schools.', 
', Grammar schools',  remain a contentious issue for the party, where there is still widespread popular support for selective education among grassroots supporters and backbenchers, particularly in the runup to the general election. Ukip has said it will allow existing schools to apply to become grammar schools.', 
', There are 164 grammars in England, with individual schools scattered across the country and others in selective areas such as Kent and Buckinghamshire, where pupils are offered places based on their abilities, which are assessed by an 11-plus examination. Those who oppose grammar schools say they are socially divisive; wealthier children are more likely to receive a place because their parents can pay for tutoring and neighbouring schools inevitably suffer.', 
', Johnson said, however, that grammar schools were \u201can important part of the educational mix \u2026 a great mobiliser and liberator for many people\u201d. He added: \u201cI\u2019m pro. I always have been,\u201d but suggested there could be changes to improve on the old \u201cbrutal\u201d 11-plus.', 
', \u201cWhether you bring back the schools called grammar schools, with academic selection or brutal academic selection at 11, maybe you don\u2019t. What I think you could do is have a greater degree of academic competition, academic selection at various stages in a child\u2019s development.', 
', \u201cThere are ways of doing that which wouldn\u2019t be hurtful to people, which wouldn\u2019t make them feel like failures, but which would spur competition and get better performance out of our schools. The principle of academic selection is, I\u2019m afraid, not one that we should jettison. There are plenty of fee-paying schools in this country that run absolutely ruthless policies of academic selection.\u201d', 
', It is not the first time Johnson has entered the grammar school debate. Last year, while delivering the Margaret Thatcher lecture to the Centre for Policy Studies, he called for a new generation of grammar schools to help the brightest children from poor homes.', 
', In a move which some commentators said could signal a change of direction in Tory education policy, the home secretary gave her support last week to a study looking into opening a \u201csatellite\u201d state-funded grammar school in her constituency. It would be the first grammar to open in a generation and avoids breaching legislation outlawing new selective schools by existing as a second \u201csatellite\u201d campus, attached to an existing grammar school in a neighbouring borough.', 
', A ', statement on May\u2019s website',  said the home secretary \u201cwelcomed proposals to consider establishing a \u2018satellite\u2019 grammar school site in Maidenhead\u201d, in response to parental demand. \u201cGrammar schools attract considerable support from Maidenhead families. If a good school wishes to expand in line with existing legislation then this must be seriously considered.\u201d', 
', Last year the former education secretary, Michael Gove, ruled out a similar proposal in Sevenoaks, Kent, after taking legal advice. The establishment of any new wholly or partially selective state-funded schools is banned under the former Labour government\u2019s School Standards and Framework Act 1998, though existing schools are allowed to expand.',

', Boris Johnson',  has become the latest senior Tory to support selective education, giving his backing to grammar schools during his LBC Radio phone-in.', 
', The London mayor, who is poised to return to Westminster after being ', selected as the Conservative party candidate at the next election for Uxbridge and South Ruislip', , described the decision to scrap grammar schools as \u201ca real tragedy for this country\u201d. \u201cI think they are a very important part of the mix in our educational system. I think that they work very well in many areas and they should be supported,\u201d he said.', 
', His comments follow an intervention last week by the home secretary, Theresa May, who backed the possibility of a new grammar school in her Maidenhead constituency in Berkshire, despite the fact that current legislation bans the creation of selective schools.', 
', Grammar schools',  remain a contentious issue for the party, where there is still widespread popular support for selective education among grassroots supporters and backbenchers, particularly in the runup to the general election. Ukip has said it will allow existing schools to apply to become grammar schools.', 
', There are 164 grammars in England, with individual schools scattered across the country and others in selective areas such as Kent and Buckinghamshire, where pupils are offered places based on their abilities, which are assessed by an 11-plus examination. Those who oppose grammar schools say they are socially divisive; wealthier children are more likely to receive a place because their parents can pay for tutoring and neighbouring schools inevitably suffer.', 
', Johnson said, however, that grammar schools were \u201can important part of the educational mix \u2026 a great mobiliser and liberator for many people\u201d. He added: \u201cI\u2019m pro. I always have been,\u201d but suggested there could be changes to improve on the old \u201cbrutal\u201d 11-plus.', 
', \u201cWhether you bring back the schools called grammar schools, with academic selection or brutal academic selection at 11, maybe you don\u2019t. What I think you could do is have a greater degree of academic competition, academic selection at various stages in a child\u2019s development.', 
', \u201cThere are ways of doing that which wouldn\u2019t be hurtful to people, which wouldn\u2019t make them feel like failures, but which would spur competition and get better performance out of our schools. The principle of academic selection is, I\u2019m afraid, not one that we should jettison. There are plenty of fee-paying schools in this country that run absolutely ruthless policies of academic selection.\u201d', 
', It is not the first time Johnson has entered the grammar school debate. Last year, while delivering the Margaret Thatcher lecture to the Centre for Policy Studies, he called for a new generation of grammar schools to help the brightest children from poor homes.', 
', In a move which some commentators said could signal a change of direction in Tory education policy, the home secretary gave her support last week to a study looking into opening a \u201csatellite\u201d state-funded grammar school in her constituency. It would be the first grammar to open in a generation and avoids breaching legislation outlawing new selective schools by existing as a second \u201csatellite\u201d campus, attached to an existing grammar school in a neighbouring borough.', 
', A ', statement on May\u2019s website',  said the home secretary \u201cwelcomed proposals to consider establishing a \u2018satellite\u2019 grammar school site in Maidenhead\u201d, in response to parental demand. \u201cGrammar schools attract considerable support from Maidenhead families. If a good school wishes to expand in line with existing legislation then this must be seriously considered.\u201d', 
', Last year the former education secretary, Michael Gove, ruled out a similar proposal in Sevenoaks, Kent, after taking legal advice. The establishment of any new wholly or partially selective state-funded schools is banned under the former Labour government\u2019s School Standards and Framework Act 1998, though existing schools are allowed to expand.',
