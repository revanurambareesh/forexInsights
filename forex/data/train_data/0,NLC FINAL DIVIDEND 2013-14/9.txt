    Public Sector in India
In the Post Independence era, India was facing severe Socio-Economic
problems like
\uf0d8regional imbalances in economic development,
\uf0d8lack of trained manpower,
\uf0d8low levels of employment,
\uf0d8weak industrial base,
\uf0d8inadequate investments,
\uf0d8lack of infrastructure facilities, etc.
\u2022 The first Prime Minister of free India, Pandit Jawaharlal Nehru and other
nationalist leaders shouldered the great responsibility of building a strong
and modern India.
\u2022 First Industrial Policy was adopted by Govt. of India in 1948 and implemented
the planned economic development polices, which envisaged the roadmap
for Public Sector.
\u2022 This resulted in self-reliant development of public sector and economic
growth of our country which paved way for creation of large base and scope
for PSUs.
August 22, 2014 Neyveli Lignite Corporation Ltd 3

    First Phase
\u2022 Initially, the public sector was confined to core and strategic
industries like Railways, Post & Telegraph, Ordinance Factories
etc. During the first five-year plan (1951-56), there were only 5
CPSEs in the country with a financial investment of Rs. 29Cr.
Second phase
Witnessed Nationalization of Banks & Industries, takeover of sick units from the
private sector and entry of the public sector into new fields.
\uf0d8 Manufacturing, Engineering, Steel, Heavy machinery, Machine tools, Fertilizers,
Drugs, Textiles, Pharmaceuticals, Petro-Chemicals, Exploration, Mining &
Extraction of Minerals, Refining of crude oil, Services such as Telecommunication,
Trading, Tourism and Warehousing and a range of consultancy services.

    Performance of CPSEs
\u2022 As a new endeavour of Govt. of India, GoI had introduced the
\u2018Navratna\u2019 scheme in 1997
\u2022 CPSEs which are at the higher end of the \u2018Navratna\u2019 category
and have the potential to become Indian Multinational
Companies (MNCs) have been recognized as a separate class
\u2018Maharatna.\u2019
\u2022 The objective of the \u2018Maharatna\u2019 scheme is to empower mega
CPSEs to expand their operations and emerge as global giants.
\u2022 Presently GoI has accorded the status of \u2018Maharatna\u2019 to 7
CPSEs, \u2018Navratna to 17 CPSEs, \u2018Miniratna Category - I CPSEs to
53 & Category - II to 18 CPSEs to empower and equip
themselves to react proactively to market forces.

    CPSEs are pioneers in CSR activities
August 22, 2014 Neyveli Lignite Corporation Ltd 9
To over come challenges in industry CSR is required.
CSR leads to good governance by balancing the need of various stakeholders and cordial Industrial Relations.
Considering importance of CSR, the Companies Act 2013 made CSR compulsory- \u201cComply or explain\u201d
But
\u2022 The importance of CSR was realised and being practiced by CPSEs for decades.
\u2022 In 1994, DPE issued Guidelines on \u201cSocial obligations of PSU\u201d
\u2022 In 2010, issued CSR guidelines directing CPSEs to spend 5% to 0.5% of Net Profit depending on profitability of the
company. ( companies earing Net Profit upto Rs 100 cr 3 to 5%, 100 cr to 500 cr \u2013 2 to 3%, above 500 cr 0.5% to 2%)
\u2022 In 2013 CSR guidelines were integrated with Sustainable development guidelines.
\u2022 MOU between the Ministries and PSUs, included CSR as one of the MOU parameters
\u2022 Thus Indian PSUs started practicing the CSR for decades, where the CSR was not a compulsory one for Indian
Corporates.
